Chapter One

The Bridge

Daniela has been at Unified Wire & Cable for five years. The first three were in order entry — the kind of job where you learn a company from the paperwork up. Then, because of her background — an industrial engineering degree — the quality team asked if she wanted to join them.

She said yes. That was two years ago. She's spent the time since learning the day-to-day of quality work, and is now training to take over Unified's entire QMS — the quality management system that governs every inspection, every test, every document in the plant.

Her path to the manager role runs through one more milestone: the annual management review. It's a wrap-up of the previous year across every department — sales, purchasing, production, quality — and this year, she's the one putting it together and presenting. After that, she expects her next conversation with the boss will be about exactly what's left between her and the role itself.

The Customer

On-time delivery. But also — and this never goes away — quality.

But she's already doing the work. The quality team at Unified holds a line between two forces that don't naturally align. Production wants to produce. Customers want their orders on time. Both also want quality — and quality, sometimes, means slowing the line down. Reconciling those pressures without alienating either side is what Daniela spends her days doing.

"I'm kind of like that bridge in between. That's the most difficult part. But it's also the most rewarding one."

Chapter Two

What "Perfect"
Means

Most people, Daniela will tell you, think quality means perfect. That is not quite right.

"Quality means understanding what perfect means," she says. "Because perfect can mean all different things."

At Unified, "perfect" is whatever the documentation says it is. A spec sheet calls out dimensions, tolerances, materials, tests. A customer specifies their own requirements on top of that. Quality's job is not to invent what good looks like — it's to translate the agreed standards into something production can hit consistently, and then verify, every day, that they do.

"We did this. Is it according to our standard? Yes. Then we can be happy about it."

— Daniela

That sounds simple. It is not. Production wants to run fast. Customers want their orders yesterday. Each has legitimate pressure. Daniela's job is to pull both sides back toward the agreed standard — repeatedly, without burning the relationship. Sometimes that means telling the production team a run can't ship. Sometimes it means telling a customer that what they received was, in fact, to spec, and any disagreement on what "spec" means needs to go back to document control. Most times, it means finding the place where everyone gets what they actually need.

Chapter Three

The Drum

Before Unified, Daniela worked across the street — at a harness shop. The job was quality, too. But the product was different. So were the stakes.

"There, it's easy to have a low PPM," she says. "Because it's per unit. You have a defective one, you just scrub it and continue."

Wire is not like that. When something goes wrong on a run at Unified, it doesn't go wrong on a single unit. It goes wrong on a drum. And one drum can be up to fifty thousand feet.

Harness Shop (Before)
1 unit
One harness fails → scrub it, continue. Low PPM comes easy.
Unified Wire (Now)
50,000 ft
One compound issue missed by thirty minutes can put a full drum on a reel that ends up scrapped or reworked.

That math is what makes Unified's quality numbers so hard-won, and it's what keeps Daniela up at night. A mislabeled drum can ship, arrive, and come back. A polymer issue that doesn't show up until the oven test seven days later can eat an entire week of production.

"It does stress me out. A lot," she says. "But I'm kind of getting used to working under pressure."

"If we have something bad, it's the whole drum. And one drum can be up to fifty thousand feet."

— Daniela
Chapter Four

Sherlock
Holmes

When a customer has a problem, Daniela's first reply is usually a request for a sample.

About ten feet, give or take. Enough to run tests on. The sample arrives at the lab. The label gets read off — which line ran the product, which shift, which operator. Then the investigation starts.

Anatomy of a Rejection — From Email to 8D
Step 1
Email. Customer flags a defect. Daniela requests a sample — roughly ten feet.
Step 2
Lab. Sample arrives. Tests run. Engineers try to reproduce the failure.
Step 3
Label traced. Line, shift, and operator identified from the drum label.
Step 4
Operator interview. Daniela translates to Spanish where needed. Not here to blame — what happened?
Step 5
Maintenance check. Often the real cause. Recently: a shaker motor misbehaving → tangle complaints from three customers in a week.
Step 6
Root cause. Daniela, the production manager, the operator — in the same room. Corrective action agreed.
Step 7
8D report written and emailed back to the customer. Loop closed.
Step 8
Material. Mexico → scrapped on-site, replacement shipped. U.S. → back to DeKalb for rework or replacement.

"I feel like Sherlock Holmes. I'm trying to find clues here and there until I put everything together."

— Daniela

Root cause, in her experience, is rarely the operator. Lately it's been the shaker — a part of the extrusion line that keeps strands from tangling. When the shaker isn't working right, strands bunch, and three or four customers suddenly ring up with tangle complaints. By the time the maintenance team has figured out why, Daniela has usually already written the 8D report and emailed it back.

Chapter Five

The Real
Answer

Ask Daniela why Unified's quality is what it is, and she'll pay the quality team a polite compliment — and then tell you the actual answer.

"Most of why we're good at quality is not the quality team," she says. "It's the operators. They've been here more than ten years. One of them, more than thirty."

She means it literally. When a rejection comes in, Daniela doesn't start with the documentation — she starts with the person at the machine. Because the person at the machine has run it long enough that they already know, before the log files do, what went slightly off.

"Some have been here more than thirty years. They really know what they're doing."

— Daniela

Why do they stay? Flexibility, first. Every first Monday of the month, all three shifts meet together in the same room. Concerns get raised. Maintenance requests go on a list. The quality team listens; so does production management. With seventy people in the company, there's not much distance between the person who runs the extruder and the person who runs the plant.

Daniela won't say "family" — she'll tell you it sounds cliché. But pressed, she'll admit that she knows just about everyone. She knows their concerns. It's what lets her pick up the phone at two on a Friday afternoon, call the operator by name, and actually get the truth.

Chapter Six

The Lab

Quality starts at the machine. It lives, day-to-day, in the lab.

Daniela and her team run the same battery of tests on a rolling daily schedule. Flame resistance. Elongation, aged and unaged. Hot crib. Gel. VW1. Emulsion testing for the wire mill's coolant tanks — samples broken down with acid to verify TFM levels and pH. Every one of those tests is a gate a spool has to pass before it becomes shippable product.

VW1 / Flame

Fire resistance. Fifteen seconds on, fifteen off, five cycles. Must self-extinguish.

Elongation

Stretch to break. Compared before and after oven aging.

Oven Aging

Seven days at standard-specified temperature. Verifies polymer integrity.

Hot Crib

Insulation deformation under heat and load.

Gel Test

Cure verification for cross-linked compounds.

Emulsion / TFM

Wire-mill coolant broken down with acid weekly. TFM and pH measured.

The Flame Test

"This one's my favorite," Daniela says. And when she shows you why, you understand.

A specimen is clamped vertically. A three-inch blue flame is applied from below for fifteen seconds, then removed for fifteen. The wire has to self-extinguish — and it has to do it before sixty seconds. Five cycles. If the flame ever reaches the paper flag taped near the top of the sample, or if burning continues past the limit on the second pass, the run fails.

The specific compound is what controls the result. If something went wrong in formulation, the flame will catch. Daniela's team runs this test every single day.

"It hasn't failed here at all."

— Daniela, on the flame test

Seven Days in the Oven

The flame test lives and dies in sixty seconds. The oven elongation test takes a week.

Two six-inch samples are cut from the same run. One goes into the oven at a temperature dictated by the applicable standard. For seven days. The other stays outside the oven as a control. At the end of the week, both samples are pulled, clamped into the elongation rig, and stretched until they snap.

The numbers have to match — within tolerance. If the aged sample elongates meaningfully less than the unaged one, it means the polymer has broken down under sustained heat. That batch doesn't ship.

What's Next

Unified is moving into medium voltage — 15kV to 69kV. That means new materials, new tests, and new compliance documents Daniela will have to write from scratch. It also means a whole new chapter of the QMS she is being trained to run.

Her quality policy, at the end of our conversation, comes in one sentence.

"On-time delivery. And products that meet the standards. That's the whole thing."

— Daniela
