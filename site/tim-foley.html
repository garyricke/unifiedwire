Chapter One

The Family
Business

Long before Tim Foley became the VP of Sales at Unified Wire & Cable, he was a kid in Wrigleyville catching rides with his older brothers to the family's harness shop. His father had started Electromechanical Devices — EMD — in 1965 on the north side of Chicago: a three-story building where they took raw wire, added terminals and connectors, and shipped wiring harness assemblies to OEM manufacturers.

"As far as my mom knew, we never touched the machines. But the truth is — there wasn't a machine in that place we didn't know how to run."

— Tim Foley, VP of Sales

His mother had one standing rule: the kids were not to touch equipment. She'd imagined fingers in terminal presses, the usual hazards of a manufacturing floor. What actually happened, Tim will tell you with a grin, is that every machine in that building eventually had a Foley kid running it.

The building was a vertical production line: wire cut on the first floor, hoisted by elevator to the second for terminal work, then up to the third for final braiding and assembly. The Foley kids learned the rhythm of the whole thing — not from training manuals, but from being there after school and on weekends, from watching and then doing.

EMD supplied harness assemblies to off-road construction equipment, agricultural machinery, and automotive OEMs. It was a customer-facing manufacturing operation entirely dependent on outside suppliers for wire. That dependence — the lead times, the quality variability, the pricing they couldn't control — planted a seed in Tim's father's mind.

Chapter Two

An Idea Called
Unified

The name wasn't accidental. "Unified" — as in unified supply chain. His father's logic was straightforward: if you're buying wire on the outside, you don't own your quality, your timing, or your cost. So why not own the wire too?

When Unified was founded in 1977, it was a single-extruder shop. An insulator — buying copper on the outside and putting plastic jackets on it. The brother, Brian, ran it. When both companies moved to DeKalb in 1983, Unified was leasing space inside EMD's building. By 1992, it had grown enough to build its own 20,000 square foot facility on the same property.

"When we moved to DeKalb in 1983, Unified was just my brother. That was all."

— Tim Foley

From that one extruder and three or four people, Unified grew year after year — expanding to 65,000 square feet, then running out of land entirely. An adjacent building became available about fifteen years ago. They expanded it and connected the two structures. Today the facility is a horseshoe-shaped complex of roughly 100,000 square feet. Seven extrusion lines. A copper fabrication operation that processes rod in-house. From the ground up.

Chapter Three

A Promise That
Became 25 Years

The harness business sold. Tim had a non-compete to honor — an 18-month window before he could re-enter that world. He talked with his brother Brian about joining Unified.

Brian's response was blunt: "You'd never learn this business in 18 months. Doesn't work for me."

Tim negotiated. He promised two years. Two years to learn the wire business, to commit fully, before he could even think about going back to harnesses.

"I promised two years. That was 25 years ago."

— Tim Foley

Brian has since sold the company — about five years ago to a group of private investors who owned Nearing, a sister company with several facilities nearby. That group then sold Nearing and Unified to Mueller Industries, a publicly traded, multi-billion dollar manufacturer based in Memphis. Tim remained through each ownership transition. He is, at this point, the institutional memory of the company in human form.

The first half of his career was harnesses — OEM applications, off-road equipment, agriculture, automotive. The second half has been wire. Between the two, he can trace the supply chain from raw copper rod all the way to a fully assembled harness shipping into a vehicle. Very few people in this industry can say that honestly.

Chapter Four

What Others
Won't Do

Ask Tim what differentiates Unified Wire, and he comes back to one word: nimble. "We do things," he'll say, "that our competition either is unable or unwilling to do." Then he tells you about last Friday.

The story goes like this: A distributor they've worked with for 30 years called at 2pm on a Friday afternoon. Their preferred supplier for a particular product had just told them — sorry, can't help you. 580,000 feet. They needed it.

"Friday, no PO. Monday noon, on a truck."

— Tim Foley

Pulling that off meant reshuffling the production schedule, which meant other customers' runs were briefly deprioritized. Unified's on-time delivery still clocks in at 95% or better. So they can occasionally absorb an emergency insertion without breaking the machine. But it takes a specific kind of organization to say yes when a 580,000-foot order arrives at 2pm on a Friday.

"Some of our competitors are pretty big machines," Tim says. "They move slowly. We sit around a table and figure out how to get it done."

Chapter Five

The
Operation

Every weekday at 8am, fifteen people gather in the conference room in DeKalb. Sales team members join remotely. They go through every single production line — what's running, what's scheduled, what's at risk. They cover sales dollars, PO intake, footage shipped, pounds shipped. They surface every customer problem that exists that morning and work through resolutions before they leave the room.

Production

Every line reviewed daily. Schedule visibility for the entire sales team.

Sales

Dollar targets, PO intake, real-time opportunities to add to active runs.

Quality

Open issues flagged and assigned. Defects tracked against the 300 ppm target.

Customer Service

Active challenges surfaced. Resolutions agreed before the meeting ends.

The 8am meeting is why a sales rep can get on a call at 9am and tell a customer exactly when their product will ship. The decision was already made in the room. No one has to go find out.

"We do things others can't or won't. And we get that opportunity quite often."

— Tim Foley

The quality numbers back it up. Unified's defective parts per million rate typically runs around 300 — meaning for every million dollars shipped, $300 worth of product comes back for a fault. "There are many of our competitors that can't compete with that," Tim says flatly.

He attributes the number to tenure. Some extrusion operators have been at Unified for more than 30 years. They don't just follow setup documentation — they have internalized the machine. They know, before the data says so, when something is slightly off. Tim calls it an art. "The equipment is sophisticated. But that product knowledge, that experience — that's what beats all the tech in the world."

Chapter Six

Still
Learning

Twenty-five years in, Tim will tell you he's still learning. The wire industry is older than most industries still operating at scale — and yet it keeps moving. Unified has spent its first decades mastering low-voltage, low-temperature hook-up wire. Now it's expanding into territory that requires entirely different expertise.

Elevator cables. Medium voltage product lines rated from 15kV to 69kV. These aren't incremental variations on existing products — they're new categories with new materials, new equipment, and new compliance requirements. Mueller Industries, as the current owner, is willing to fund that expansion. The investment is committed.

"Sometimes they're surprised to hear we make that. Because they thought of the old Unified — just an insulator of skinny wire. That's how we started. But that's not what we are."

— Tim Foley

That's part of why the website matters to Tim. The company has outgrown its own reputation. Long-time customers sometimes don't know about entire product lines Unified has been running for years. New customers find them on the web and expect a widget vendor. The gap between what Unified is and what people think it is — that's the marketing problem he's trying to solve.

And as for the growth numbers: Unified has never failed to grow year over year. Tim says it without hesitation. The past two weeks at the plant produced more volume in dollars, pounds, and footage than the entire year did when he joined. "That will continue," he says. "With our new ownership, we're not afraid to invest where it makes sense."
